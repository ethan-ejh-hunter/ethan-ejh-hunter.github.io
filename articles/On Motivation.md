## On Motivation
I just barely finished one of my first long personal projects (~100 hours or so of personal time). I'm currently taking a CPU design class, making a simplified RISC-V processor on a Xilinx FPGA. And by simplified, for instance, we don't have instructions to read or write anything smaller than a word (so no reading/writing to a particular byte or halfword), and obviously no hardware multiply or divide. I decided to write a Zork Z-Machine interpreter for it, entirely in RISC-V assembly. And I wanted to write out some of my thoughts that made this a project that I actually finished. 

The principle I tried to work by is this: motivation is *the* currency in personal projects, spend it well. Some pieces of your project are investments, and some are fixed costs. Ruthlessly protect your investments. 

This has two major applications. The first is with AI use. Some pieces of your project are just not terribly interesting or fun. Sometimes, that's boilerplate. Sometimes, it's the pieces of tooling you don't enjoy. Often, for me, it's something simple like "I don't like the way I named literally all of my functions, I want them to use this pattern instead." Those are prime candidates for delegating to AI, especially agentic AI. If it's something that takes time and motivation to do, but doesn't leave you feeling some level of satisfaction or excitement after, it can be really helpful to hand it off. 

My typical rule was that I considered myself to be the engineer, just as in any company. So, it was important that I understand all of the code I produce. It's important for me to have my hand intimately in both the high level and low level design of my code. But, as with most engineers, it's not important that I perfectly understand every tool I use. Some other engineer in the company is responsible for the upkeep and design of that tool. So, I insisted that I write all important code myself, but for me tooling wasn't very fun to write. There were tons of little scripts that needed to be written to parse the Z-code file to provide ground truth for what my RISC-V code should be parsing about particular objects. But I didn't find writing those scripts interesting like I did writing the assembly code, so I just didn't write it.

The second major application is actively choosing where you invest more or less of your personal time. In programming, there are almost always multiple ways to solve a particular issue. You can spend time thinking about an issue, and optimizing it, and eventually end up with something clever. There are also ways that are simple and require much less thought, and perhaps solve it a little less elegantly. You may be thinking I plan to tell you to avoid optimizing and coming up with clever solutions. That is not what I'm saying. Rather, I'm saying that you need to be "motivation-maxing" when you're optimizing. Let me give two examples. The first is a time when I chose to dig into the optimization, and the second when I purposely chose not to. 

Z-Machine code, if you haven't heard about it before, is a bytecode. It's a CISC bytecode, with arbitrary length instructions that can go from like 2 bytes to 9 bytes. And then different instruction forms store the types of operands very differently. I had made this nice routine for parsing a single operand, given you tell it what type of operand it should be looking for. I had this odd obsession in this project with avoiding conditionals--I liked doing clever bit fiddling methods to avoid jumps and branches and such, and there was likely some (small) performance impact. So I spent probably 10 or 15 minutes or so messing with the bits, trying to find the cleverest way to transmute the way that one instruction stores the operand types into the way that my function stores operand types, without just doing a set of if blocks. 

To me, this was worth it. I sat back at the end and was like, "wow, that's a clever piece of code." I mean, it was a pain and definitely not worth the performance gain. But I felt clever, it was technically faster, and I had a win under my belt, that earned back some motivation.

But it's not always worth it. The Z-Machine has a single opcode that handles collecting a line of user input and parsing the entire thing — dictionary lookup included. That's a lot for one opcode, though Z-Machine is essentially a DSL for text adventures, so it makes a certain sense. Once you've collected input, you parse it by going through the dictionary, looking up each word, and returning its index. The dictionary is technically binary-searchable since it's in alphabetical order, but entries are 9 bytes each, and on this CPU reading individual bytes required helper routines rather than simple word reads. I just didn't want to debug that. So I wrote a naive linear search that starts at the top and checks every entry, and it worked first try.

In other words, that old adage about "beware of premature optimization" is true, but this is *your* project. If you really enjoy writing clever little bit twiddling routines, or throwing together good data-structures and efficient O(n) algorithms to parse them, do it. But, sometimes, if you find you don't enjoy a part very much, it only has to be "good enough."

So, I finished my first long term personal project, and I'm already digging into my next: reverse engineering as much of Outrun 2006 as I can, both for fun and to learn more about Ghidra (you can find my current logs and documented functions, classes, structs, and globals elsewhere on this blog). And honestly, the motivation currency is already spending and earning in familiar ways. Some parts of reverse engineering are genuinely thrilling — the "oh, that was so clever" moment in reading someone else's code, the moment a function starts to make sense, or you realize what a class is actually doing. Others are just tedious bookkeeping. You already know what I do with those.
